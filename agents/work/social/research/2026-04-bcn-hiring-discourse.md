## 3. Regulatory Landscape

### 3.1 EU AI Act — New Compliance Roles

The EU AI Act classifies most employment-related AI systems as **high-risk**, including tools for recruitment, job ad placement, application filtering, and candidate evaluation. This creates several concrete implications:

**New role categories emerging**:
- AI Compliance Specialist (EverAI is hiring Junior/Mid positions, fully remote in Spain)
- Responsible AI Advisor (Accenture, Madrid/Barcelona, 8+ years required)
- AI Governance Responsible (Talan, Madrid, 8+ years required)
- Chief AI Officer (CAIO) — a role that barely existed two years ago, distinct from CTO

**Compliance costs**: Estimated €150,000–€500,000 per AI-deploying scale-up for algorithmic auditing and documentation. This is not marginal for a Series B company with a 30-month cash runway.

**Technical obligations for high-risk AI systems**:
- Data quality controls and event logging
- Traceability and human oversight mechanisms
- Security measures integrated into AI pipelines from development
- Employers must inform workers and representatives about AI use in advance
- Prohibited: biometric categorization, emotion inference in the workplace, personality-based risk profiling

**[Inference]**: The EU AI Act is creating a new professional category — "AI compliance engineering" — that combines legal knowledge with technical implementation. This category barely exists in Barcelona's talent market. Dídac's combination of banking regulatory experience + ML/AI specialization positions him to enter this emerging space, particularly at CaixaBank Tech or fintech firms where AI compliance intersects with financial regulation.

### 3.2 Spanish Teletrabajo Law (Ley 10/2021)

**Core provisions**:
- Applies when ≥30% of work is performed remotely over a 3-month reference period.
- Requires a written remote work agreement *before* work begins, specifying: equipment inventory, expense reimbursement, working hours, availability schedules, work location, digital disconnection protocols.
- Remote work cannot be imposed unilaterally — both parties must agree.
- Employers must provide or compensate direct/indirect remote work costs (energy, internet, supplies).

**Tech sector adoption**:
- Remote work in tech/financial/consulting sectors exceeds 50%, vs. ~14% of Spain's total workforce.
- Q1 2025: 37.4% of companies with 10+ employees permit remote work; 78% of companies with 250+ employees.

**2026 regulatory changes in effect**:
- Mandatory digital time tracking systems (paper/Excel no longer valid).
- Right to digital disconnection outside working hours.

**37.5-hour workweek proposal**: The Ministerio de Trabajo's proposal to reduce the legal maximum from 40 to 37.5 hours/week was **rejected** by Congress in September 2025. The 40-hour maximum remains in effect.

Source: [Ikelma — Teletrabajo en España 2026](https://www.ikelma.com/blog/teletrabajo-espana-guia-completa)
Source: [Remote Laws — Spain 2026](https://remotelaws.com/remote-work-laws/countries/europe/spain/)

### 3.3 RD 902/2020 and EU Pay Transparency Directive

**RD 902/2020** (in effect since April 2021):
- Mandatory pay register for all companies.
- Gender-neutral pay audits required for companies with equality plans.
- Equal pay for work of equal value, implementing Art. 28 of the Workers' Statute.

**EU Pay Transparency Directive (2023/970)** — Spain must transpose by **7 June 2026**:
- Job postings must include specific salary or pay ranges based on objective, gender-neutral criteria.
- Employers **cannot ask candidates about salary history**.
- Employees can request individual pay levels and average pay by gender for equivalent roles.
- Companies with 250+ employees: annual gender pay gap reporting.
- Companies with 150–249 employees: every three years.
- If unexplained gaps exceed 5%: mandatory joint pay assessment and corrective measures within six months.
- **Burden of proof shifts to employers** to demonstrate pay differences are based on objective criteria.

Current wage gap in Spain: 15.74% (INE data); women earn an average of €4,781 less annually.

**[Inference]**: The pay transparency directive is directly beneficial for career changers entering tech. Mandatory salary bands in job postings eliminate information asymmetry. The prohibition on asking salary history prevents anchoring to lower non-tech salaries. For Dídac specifically, this means employers cannot use his banking/insurance salary history to low-ball a tech offer.

Source: [Payslip — EU Pay Transparency Directive: What It Means for Spain](https://payslip.com/resources/blog/eu-pay-transparency-directive-what-it-means-for-spain)
Source: [Europa Press — Cuenta atrás para la ley de transparencia salarial](https://www.europapress.es/eseuropa/noticia-comunicado-cuenta-atras-ley-transparencia-salarial-obligaciones-clave-antes-junio-2026-20260407100811.html)

### 4.2 Cultural Attitudes Toward Career Changers

**Positive signals**:

- Spain's bootcamp ecosystem (Ironhack, KeepCoding, Hack a Boss, Adalab, ITAcademy) has normalized career transitions into tech. Ironhack Barcelona reports 94% employment rate 180 days after completion, above their global average. Adalab reports 90% insertion rate across 10 cohorts.

**Negative signals**:

- Seed funding decline (−34% YoY) is starving the early-stage companies that traditionally absorb and develop junior/career-change talent. The pipeline is "thinning from the bottom" (KiTalent).

**[Inference]**: The cultural landscape is contradictory. Official discourse celebrates career changers (bootcamp marketing, government programs, press features). The hiring data shows real age bias beyond 40. At 36, Dídac is positioned in the cultural sweet spot — young enough to avoid the age cliff, old enough to leverage experience credibly. ## 5. Recruiter Red/Green Flags

### 5.1 CV Red Flags — What Gets Candidates Rejected

RemoDevs (European tech recruitment firm, February 2026 update) identifies three primary red flags in developer CVs, based on a 90% candidate rejection rate:

**Red Flag 1: Responsibilities instead of achievements**

| Red 🚩 | Green 🟢 |
|--------|----------|
| "Responsible for maintaining the codebase" | "Reduced API response time by 40% by refactoring legacy queries" |
| "Worked with Java and Spring Boot" | "Led migration of monolith to microservices, supporting 10K+ concurrent users" |
| "Attended daily Scrum meetings" | "Automated deployment pipelines, cutting release time from 2 days to 3 hours" |

**Red Flag 2: Job hopping without context**

Positions lasting <12–18 months repeatedly raise concerns about onboarding ROI (3–6 months to full productivity), depth of work, and cultural fit. However, honest explanations (layoffs, tech stack changes, contract work) are accepted.

**Red Flag 3: Buzzword-stuffed skills sections without proof of depth**

Listing 15+ languages/frameworks without corresponding project evidence. Claiming "AI/ML" or "Blockchain" as skills without portfolio proof. Recruiters seek a **T-shaped profile**: deep expertise in 1–2 areas supported by broad understanding of related tools.

### 5.4 Green Flags Recruiters Explicitly Seek in Spain

Synthesized from RemoDevs, Manfred, beon.tech, and Git to Hire:

1. **Quantified achievements** over responsibilities — "built X that resulted in Y% improvement."
2. **Consistent GitHub activity** — regular commits proving habitual engineering practice.
3. **T-shaped skill profile** — deep in 1–2 areas (e.g., Python + ML), broad in related tools.
4. **Deployed, usable projects** — not tutorials; real systems with real users (even if that user is yourself).
5. **Technical blog posts or documentation** — 3+ posts explaining decisions and tradeoffs.
6. **Tenure stability** — 2+ years at previous roles, or clear explanation for shorter stints.
7. **READMEs with problem statements, technical decisions, and setup instructions** — signals engineering maturity.
8. **Video walkthroughs** of projects (2–3 minutes) — increase recruiter engagement by 42%.

### 5.5 Red Flags Recruiters Explicitly Avoid

1. **Vague responsibilities** without measurable outcomes.
2. **Buzzword-stuffed skills** without project evidence ("AI/ML, Blockchain, Cloud" with no corresponding work).
3. **No GitHub/portfolio link** — in 2026, absence of public code is increasingly disqualifying.
4. **Job hopping** (<12 months × 3+ positions) without explanation.
5. **Birth date or age on CV** — Manfred explicitly advises against this: "Poner la fecha de nacimiento en los CV no sirve para nada. Crea sesgos."
6. **Tutorial-clone projects** (to-do apps, weather apps) — signal following instructions, not engineering capability.
